Away Daze

Sam Hardwicke November 5, 2018 Away Days Copa Paulista Juventus Sao Paulo

It’s 8 am on a Sunday morning and the sun is shining brightly on the crowd outside the ‘Onion bar’ in Mooca, a barrio in the Central-West district of Sao Paulo. The Onion is home to Sector 2, a group of C.A. Juventus fans who have welcomed me with open arms since I first found my way to a Juve game around a year ago. A group of around 40 or so fans have gathered here to take the two-hour bus ride North to Atibaia, a conurbation of around 125,000 people, for an important game in the Copa Paulista (a tournament for sides in the state of Sao Paulo who are not part of the national set-up).

Away games for Juventus are usually within city limits (and reachable by public transport) or several hundred kilometres away.  As a result, bus trips such as these are somewhat rare and excitement levels are high: many of the beers marked for consumption en route have already disappeared and large glasses of industrial-flavoured cachaca are doing the rounds. Everyone is in high spirits. But weren’t we supposed to be leaving already? The bus driver seems to be the only one keen to get going. Eventually, more beers and ice arrive, the big bass drum is loaded onto the bus and we are on our way.

The party starts immediately and for a Sunday morning I must say it is a sight, and sound, to behold.  For the duration of the journey, the seats are treated like the terraces at Rua Javari, Juventus’ home, as the group bounce and sway through a repertoire of songs seemingly aimed at insulting every other club in Sao Paulo one after the other.  Moving slowly through the notorious city traffic, the bus is greeted with no shortage of honks, cheers and waves from passers-by. Heavy smoke fills the air, thankfully masking the smell from the toilet (passengers who force their way through the throng are urged not to flush for fear of making it worse), the drum beat pounds away and as we make our way into the countryside, light fittings and luggage racks begin to fall from the ceiling, much to the amusement of those underneath.

As we approach Atibaia, I check the time on my phone and am alarmed to note that kick-off time was 15 minutes ago! I turn to inform Joao, stood next to me, but he simply beams at me as he puts his arm around my shoulder: ‘Yes we are missing the game,’ he chuckles, ‘but we knew this would happen before we left The Onion.’ It takes me a few moments to process this. I glance around at the swaying, smiling, care-free faces in front of me and am reminded of a familiar feeling I have experienced many times before in situations such as these: sure, the game is important, but there is something more powerful here, a togetherness, a bond formed by the collective expression of emotion that occurs when passionate people gather together over something they love.

I feel this sensation once more when, after the game, I remark to Fernando, one of the leaders of Sector 2, how well Juventus played today. “Yeah?” he replies, looking vaguely pleased with this suggestion, for he has no idea what happened on the pitch. Like others around him, his match was spent facing his fellow supporters, bouncing up and down, arms pumping rhythmically in time with the chanting. Expressing his love for the beautiful game.
