Party at the Pacaembu?

Chris Blair October 8, 2018 Pacaembu

Most football fans will have a first memory of when they fell in love with the samba football of the Brazil national team. Maybe it was the 70s teams with Pele and the boys. Perhaps it was Romario skipping it into the net in 1994, or four years later when Ronaldo and his gang knocked a ball around an airport. For me it was around 1996 when I was gifted a history of Brazilian football on VHS. That tape was worn down to the nub by the end of the year, and a love of the greats of Brazilian football was born. Somewhere on that video was grainy footage of the 1950 World Cup held in Brazil: the 199,854 people packed into the Maracana, the skills of Balthazar, Zizinho and Ademir, the heartbreak of a nation losing out to Uruguay. One of the host stadiums that year was a then 10-year-old Pacaembu. Fast-forward almost 70 years and the old stadium is still going strong. It was here that we set off to watch Santos v Gremio last Thursday. One of the great homes of Brazilian football hosting the team of Pele - what more could one want?

This was Santos’ first return to the stadium since being eliminated from the Copa Libertadores in controversial circumstances. The ensuing riot meant that police presence was high, but a friendly enough atmosphere was present inside and outside the ground. The 7pm kickoff meant that the ground slowly filled up as the first-half progressed and, to be honest, those arriving late missed very little. The same could be said if someone had rocked up after the final whistle. Bar a potential home penalty towards the end, this was a turgid late-season affair. Gremio, with title aspirations now dented, looked the more technically proficient without ever looking dangerous. The current continental champions did nothing to suggest that their exit from the competition last month was undeserved, and it leaves the race at the top with three main contenders.
