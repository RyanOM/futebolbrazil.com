The Times They Are A Changin’

Chris Blair October 8, 2018 Copa Libertadores Palmeiras

Watching your team live is unbeatable. From waking up until kick off your day revolves around that anticipation of the match- often surpassing the quality of football on show. However, watching football in brazil offers some vastly contrasting options when it comes to the period of time between that waking up and the first ball being played- and it’s not lazy weekend mornings after one too many caipirinhas that I’m talking about here. This past week I attended two Palmeiras games one at 11am on Sunday and one at 9.45pm on Wednesday. With scheduling that makes the complaints of Friday night football and lunchtime kickoffs in the UK look churlish I decided to investigate what was at the route of the unorthodox timing of games.


A passionate football country Brazil also has a guilty secret- telenovellas! For the unacquainted think Eastenders with a touch of panache and a heap more attractiveness. A focal point of Brazilian daily life these tend to start around 7 and finish around 10pm. So, the land of sexy football has decided that to accommodate their other great televisual passion football should make the move- an idea that a redent arrival to Sao Paulo from London greeted with enthusiasm “ finally a country that appreciates soaps more than football!”.


So, as I write this with heavy eyes, my visit to Allianz Parque to watch Palmeiras secure their place in the semis of the Copa Libertadores, meant getting home at 12:30 am and that was without hitting a boteco to celebrate (although we did get to watch the penalties between Bahia and Botafogo in our taxi as it hurtled to our neighborhood – note to anyone trying this….make sure your driver doesn’t support one of the teams playingas it makes for a rollercoaster of a journey home!!


The 11 am kickoff is a bit better on the sleep patterns and brings a more family oriented crowd- although I think the guys behind us on the terraces took it as a chance to catch a game on the way home from a night out! The upside of this seem to be that in a country where Sunday lunch at mum’s is still a must you can catch a game the downside, for players and pasty faced scots, is that the game takes place in the searing midday heat. At the Pacaembu this proved a particular problem seeing as there is no escape on the terraces.


So, the long and the short of it is that watching football in Brazil is great fun – day or night- but don’t expect much sleep!
